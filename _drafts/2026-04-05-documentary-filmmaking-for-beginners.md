Documentary filmmaking is the most accessible genre for beginners. You don't need actors, sets, or a screenplay. You need a subject you care about, a camera, and the willingness to listen.

This guide covers everything you need to go from idea to finished documentary.

## Finding Your Subject

The best beginner documentaries focus on something you already have access to:

- A family member with an interesting story
- A local business, artist, or craftsperson
- A community issue you care about
- A personal journey or challenge
- A subculture or hobby you're part of

Start with proximity and access. A compelling subject you can film every day beats a fascinating subject you can only film once.

**The question test:** Can you articulate a central question your documentary explores? "What does it mean to..." or "How does someone..." If you have a question, you have a documentary.

## Story Structure for Documentaries

Even documentaries need structure. The most common formats:

**Character-driven:** Follow a person through a journey with a clear beginning (who they are), middle (what they face), and end (how they change or don't). Most beginner docs work best in this format.

**Issue-driven:** Explore a topic through multiple perspectives. Requires more research and access to different viewpoints.

**Observational (verite):** The camera observes without narration or interviews. Requires patience and extensive footage. Hard for beginners but powerful when it works.

**Essay/personal:** Your voice guides the narrative. Combines footage, interviews, and your perspective. Works well for personal subjects.

## Interview Techniques

Interviews are the backbone of most documentaries. Getting good interviews is a skill:

**Setup:**
- Place the subject near a window for natural light
- Set the camera slightly to one side — subject looks at you (just beside the lens), not into the camera
- Use a quiet room. Turn off AC, fridge, anything that hums
- Frame at medium close-up (chest and up)

**Asking questions:**
- Ask open-ended questions ("Tell me about..." not "Did you like...")
- Stay silent after they answer — people fill silence with deeper thoughts
- Ask "How did that make you feel?" for emotional depth
- Ask them to include the question in their answer ("It made me feel..." instead of just "Sad")
- Don't be afraid of pauses. The best moments often come after the "official" answer

**Technical tips:**
- Record at least 30 seconds of room tone
- Run audio levels before the interview — check for clipping
- White balance and lock exposure before starting
- Use [FrameCoach](https://framecoach.io) to get your camera settings right before the interview so you can focus entirely on the conversation

## Essential Gear for Documentary

You need less than you think:

**Camera:** Your phone, a DSLR/mirrorless, or even a webcam. Content matters more than resolution in documentary.

**Audio (most important):** A lavalier mic ($20-50) for interviews. A shotgun mic ($60+) for ambient sound. Never rely on camera audio alone.

**Tripod:** Interviews need stable framing. A $30 tripod is fine.

**Lighting:** One LED panel ($30-50) as a key light, or just use window light. Documentary doesn't need three-point lighting — it needs to look natural.

**Storage:** Documentaries generate enormous amounts of footage. Budget for large SD cards and a backup hard drive.

## Shooting a Documentary

### Shoot Ratios
Expect a 20:1 to 50:1 shooting ratio (20-50 minutes of footage for every minute in the final cut). This is normal. Shoot generously.

### B-Roll Strategy
For every interview, shoot B-roll of:
- The subject doing what they talk about
- Their environment (home, workplace, neighborhood)
- Details and textures (hands, tools, objects mentioned)
- Wide establishing shots of locations

B-roll makes or breaks a documentary. Without it, you have talking heads. With it, you have a visual story.

### Verite Footage
Beyond interviews and B-roll, capture unscripted moments:
- The subject interacting naturally with others
- Unexpected events
- Quiet moments between action

Keep the camera rolling longer than you think necessary. Documentary gold often appears in the moments you almost stopped recording.

## Editing Your Documentary

Documentary editing is where the story truly gets built. You're not following a script — you're constructing narrative from raw material.

**Step 1: Transcribe everything.** Use free tools (Otter.ai, YouTube auto-captions) to transcribe all interviews. Edit on paper first — identify the best quotes and group them by theme.

**Step 2: Build a paper edit.** Arrange your best quotes into a narrative arc. This is your rough structure.

**Step 3: Assemble.** Lay down the interview segments in order. Don't worry about B-roll yet.

**Step 4: Add B-roll.** Cover interview audio with relevant visuals. Every time the subject mentions something, show it.

**Step 5: Add music and ambient sound.** Music sets the emotional tone. Use royalty-free music (Artlist, Epidemic Sound, or free options from YouTube Audio Library).

**Step 6: Cut ruthlessly.** Your first assembly will be too long. Cut anything that doesn't serve the central question. If a quote is good but off-topic, cut it.

## Legal Considerations

- Get signed release forms from everyone who appears on camera
- Be transparent about your intentions with subjects
- Fact-check claims made in interviews
- Use royalty-free or licensed music only
- Blur faces and alter voices if subjects request anonymity

## Distribution

Finished your doc? Options for getting it seen:

- **Film festivals:** Start with local festivals, then submit to larger ones via FilmFreeway
- **YouTube/Vimeo:** Free distribution, largest potential audience
- **Local screenings:** Host a screening at a community center, library, or café
- **Social media:** Cut a 60-second trailer for Instagram/TikTok

Your first documentary doesn't need to win Sundance. It needs to exist. The act of completing a documentary — from concept through edit — teaches you more than any course or tutorial. Start with what's in front of you, and tell the story only you can tell.
