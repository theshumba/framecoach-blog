Dialogue scenes are the backbone of narrative filmmaking. They seem simple — just two people talking — but they're where many beginner filmmakers struggle most. The difference between a flat dialogue scene and a compelling one comes down to camera placement, coverage strategy, and editing rhythm.

This guide breaks down exactly how to shoot a dialogue scene that keeps your audience engaged.

## The 180-Degree Line Rule

Before you place a single camera, understand the 180-degree line (also called the axis of action). Draw an imaginary line between your two characters. All your camera positions should stay on the same side of that line.

Why? Because crossing the line disorients the viewer. Character A, who was screen-left, suddenly appears screen-right. The audience loses spatial awareness, and the scene feels wrong even if they can't articulate why.

You *can* break this rule intentionally — crossing the line creates a jarring effect that works for confrontations or psychological shifts. But learn to follow it before you break it.

## The Master Shot

Start every dialogue scene with a **master shot** — a wide angle that shows both characters and their environment. Shoot the entire scene from this angle, start to finish.

The master shot serves two purposes:
1. It establishes spatial relationships for the audience
2. It gives your editor a safety net — any time a cut doesn't work, they can cut back to the master

Place the camera at roughly the midpoint of the 180-degree line, far enough back to see both characters with some breathing room.

## Coverage Strategy: The Standard Setup

The classic dialogue coverage uses three setups:

**1. Master shot** — Both characters, full scene
**2. Over-the-shoulder on Character A** — Camera behind B, focused on A
**3. Over-the-shoulder on Character B** — Camera behind A, focused on B

For each OTS shot, you can vary the tightness:
- **Medium close-up** (chest up) for normal conversation
- **Close-up** (face only) for emotional intensity
- **Extreme close-up** (eyes) for critical moments

Shoot each setup for the entire scene. Yes, the entire scene — not just that character's lines. Reactions are often more important than dialogue delivery.

## Single vs Two-Camera

If you have two cameras, you can shoot both OTS angles simultaneously. This saves time and gives actors continuity — they're playing the scene once with a real scene partner instead of performing to an empty chair.

But single-camera has advantages: you can light each angle independently (better control) and you can reposition the camera for more dynamic framing.

For most indie productions, single-camera is the reality. That's fine — Hollywood shot single-camera for decades.

## Lens Choice for Dialogue

The standard dialogue lens range is **50mm to 85mm** (full frame equivalent). Here's why:

- **50mm** — Neutral perspective, feels natural, works for master and medium shots
- **85mm** — Slight compression, flattering for faces, perfect for close-ups
- **35mm** — Wider, shows more environment, good for establishing spatial dynamics

Avoid anything wider than 35mm for close-ups — wide lenses distort faces. Avoid anything longer than 100mm unless you want a voyeuristic, compressed feel (which can work for surveillance or thriller scenes).

## Camera Height and Eye Line

Place the camera at the eye level of the character being filmed. This is the neutral, most natural angle.

Variations:
- **Slightly below eye level** — Makes the character feel powerful, dominant
- **Slightly above eye level** — Makes the character feel vulnerable, small
- **Matching heights** — Creates equality between characters

These subtle angle changes communicate power dynamics without a word of dialogue.

## Movement in Dialogue Scenes

Static dialogue scenes aren't bad — many great films are shot locked-off. But movement adds energy:

- **Slow push-in** during an emotional revelation
- **Slow pull-back** as a character withdraws emotionally
- **Tracking with a walking conversation** (Aaron Sorkin's signature)
- **Handheld** for intimacy or tension

The key: every movement should have a reason. Movement for its own sake is distracting.

## Audio Considerations

Dialogue scenes live or die on audio quality. A few essentials:

- **Boom mic** overhead, just out of frame, is the gold standard
- **Lavalier mics** on both actors as backup
- **Room tone**: record 30 seconds of silence in the location for editing
- **Overlap**: let actors' lines overlap naturally — your editor will love you for the options

## The Edit Cuts Everything Together

Shoot more coverage than you think you need. In the edit, you'll discover that the best version of the scene might use a completely different rhythm than you imagined on set.

General editing guidelines for dialogue:
- Cut to the listener during important lines (reaction > delivery)
- Use the master shot to reset spatial awareness
- Let moments breathe — don't cut on every line
- Match eye lines across cuts

## Practical Checklist

Before rolling on a dialogue scene:
1. Identify the 180-degree line
2. Shoot the master (full scene)
3. Shoot OTS on each character (full scene each)
4. Get at least one close-up per character
5. Shoot inserts (hands, objects, environment details)
6. Record room tone
7. Review playback for eye-line consistency

The more coverage options you give your editor, the better your scene will be. Dialogue scenes are won in the edit, but you need the right footage to win with.
