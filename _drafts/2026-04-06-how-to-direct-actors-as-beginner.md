Directing actors is the skill most beginner filmmakers avoid. You feel comfortable behind the camera but freeze when an actor asks, "What's my motivation?" This guide gives you practical tools to direct performances confidently — even if you've never done it before.

## The Director's Job Is Not Acting

You don't need to be a great actor to direct great performances. Your job is to:
1. Know what the scene needs emotionally
2. Communicate that clearly
3. Create an environment where the actor feels safe to perform

That's it. You're a guide, not a performer.

## Before the Shoot: Preparation

### Know the Script Cold
Understand every scene's purpose in the larger story. For each scene, answer:
- What does each character want?
- What's preventing them from getting it?
- How do they feel at the start vs the end of the scene?
- What's the scene's function in the overall story?

### Have a Conversation, Not a Rehearsal
Meet your actors before shoot day. Don't rehearse lines — discuss the character. Ask them:
- How do you see this character?
- What do you think they want in this scene?
- Is there anything about the character you're unsure about?

Listen more than you talk. Actors often have insights about characters that you missed.

## On Set: Directing Techniques

### Use Verbs, Not Adjectives
The most important directing technique: give direction as actions, not emotions.

**Bad direction:** "Be sadder in this take."
**Good direction:** "This time, try to hold it together. Don't let them see you're hurt."

**Bad:** "Be more angry."
**Good:** "You've been patient for an hour. This is the moment you stop being patient."

Verbs give actors something to *do*. Adjectives give them something to *indicate*, which produces fake-looking performances.

### The "As If" Technique
Relate the scene to something the actor can connect with personally:

"Play this as if you just found out your best friend has been lying to you for a year."

"Deliver this line as if you're trying to convince yourself, not them."

This gives actors an emotional anchor without requiring them to manufacture feelings.

### Adjustments, Not Overhauls
After a take, give one specific adjustment:

"That was great. This time, let's try it where you don't look at them until the last line."

"Love what you're doing. Can you try it faster — like you don't have time to think about what you're saying?"

One note per take. Multiple notes confuse actors and make them self-conscious.

### The Magic of "Do Less"
When an actor is overperforming (which is common with inexperienced actors), don't say "tone it down." Instead:

"You're doing great. Now try it where you're trying to hide what you're feeling."

Or simply: "Perfect. Now do it again, but throw it away — like it's no big deal."

Restraint reads as authenticity on camera. The camera amplifies everything.

### Give Actors Permission to Fail
The best performances come from actors who aren't afraid to look stupid. Create that safety:

"Let's just try something weird — if it doesn't work, we'll go back to what we had."

"Don't worry about getting it perfect. Let's play with it."

When actors feel safe, they take risks. Risks produce the moments that make films memorable.

## Technical Considerations

### Eye Lines
Tell actors exactly where to look. In dialogue, they should look at their scene partner's eyes (or the mark where the partner would be in single coverage). A wandering eye line breaks the scene.

### Continuity
Track what actors are doing with their hands, when they sit/stand, which direction they turn. Matching action across cuts is critical. Assign someone (even yourself) to watch for continuity between takes.

### Camera Awareness
Actors should never look at the camera (unless that's the intention). If an actor keeps glancing at the lens, move the camera further away and use a longer lens — this gives them more distance from the mechanical presence of the camera.

### Sound Overlaps
In dialogue scenes, have actors overlap their lines naturally. Unnatural pausing between lines sounds like a school play. Your editor can always cut to create pauses, but they can't create natural-sounding overlap.

## Working with Non-Actors

If you're casting friends or non-professionals:

- Give them simple, physical directions ("walk to the window, then turn around")
- Avoid emotional direction — instead, put them in situations that produce the emotion naturally
- Use real conversations instead of scripted dialogue when possible
- Shoot many takes — non-actors get more natural the more they repeat something
- Create a relaxed environment. Music between takes helps.

Tools like [FrameCoach](https://framecoach.io) can handle the technical camera decisions, freeing your mental bandwidth to focus on what matters most: the human performance in front of the lens.

## Common Mistakes

**Directing every line.** Give actors space. Direct the overall scene, then let them perform. Only step in when something specific needs adjustment.

**Giving results instead of actions.** "Be funnier" is useless. "Try to impress them — you're showing off" gives the actor something to play.

**Not shooting enough takes.** Actors warm up. The first take is usually finding the scene. By take 3-4, they're in it. Don't move on after two takes unless you're certain.

**Neglecting reaction shots.** Direct both the speaker and the listener. The listener's reaction is often more important than the delivery.

## The One Rule That Matters

Respect your actors. They're putting themselves in a vulnerable position — performing emotions in front of a crew, being recorded, trying to bring your vision to life. Thank them. Be patient. Give clear direction. And when they nail it, tell them.

A director who makes actors feel valued and safe will always get better performances than a technically brilliant director who makes them feel small.
